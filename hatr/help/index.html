This Unicode character app allows you to produce or analyse runs of Template text using the Template script. Character apps are especially useful for people who don't know a script well, as characters are displayed in ways that aid identification.

If something is broken or missing raise an issue. For release notes see the Github commit list. The pictures of the UI used in this page are taken from a variety of character apps.

Inputting characters

The app includes characters in the Unicode Adlam block that are used for writing text in the Adlam script.

To analyse text, copy paste it into the large text area and select the desired control.

To produce Template text in the text area, do one of the following:

  • Type on the keyboard, using type-assist. A quick way to input text from ASCII characters on a keyboard, based on a Latin transcription.
  • Open the shape-based input, and click on similar shapes to highlight characters you may be looking for. Good when you don't know the orthography well.
  • Click on the characters in the selection area. The blocks are arranged to assist with input, and groups of characters are arranged within a block phonetically. When you mouse over the shapes, the character app displays the joining forms just above. Green characters are in the extended repertoire used for foriegn sounds.

These are all described in more detail below.

Click on FORMAT CHARS to access and input formatting characters to control direction, etc., including the Arabic tatweel.

For the latest glyph shapes choose the Noto Sans Adlam New font (can be set for the text area and for the selection panel). The font named simply Noto Sans Adlam uses older glyph shapes.

Controls above the text area

Transliterate. Converts the contents of the text area to a Latin transliteration that provides a one-to-one correspondence between Template and Latin symbols.

The transliteration scheme was developed for this character app, and, where possible, uses symbols that represent sounds typically associated with their Template equivalents, rather than the sometimes fairly arbitrary selection of diacritics found in other systems. Of course, the complexities of mapping graphemes to sounds means that this is far from a real phonetic transcription, and it doesn't show inherent vowels, but the process is reversible (unlike most transcriptions).

Transliterations produced this way are particularly useful for understanding the composition of text in scripts, where the individual parts are not well separated in the text.

Make vocab. This helps speed up the creation of entries for the vocabulary app. You can create a vocab item with up to 3 parts, delimited by |, in the following order: [1] native text, [2] meaning, [3] notes (optional). Much of the time, only the first 2 items are needed.

The text will be replaced with the following:

native_text|meaning|IPA_transcription[|notes]

You may need to minimally tweak the auto-generated text.

ᐅᒥᐊᖅ|boat

will produce:

ᐅᒥᐊᖅ|boat|umiaq

The app will automatically generate a Latin transcription in parentheses if the Latin orthography differs from the IPA.

ᐅᒥᐊᖓ|his boat

will produce:

ᐅᒥᐊᖓ|his boat|umiaŋa (umianga)

Here's an example where notes are provided.

ᓇᑯᕐᒦᒃ|thank you|South Qikiqtaaluk

will produce:

ᓇᑯᕐᒦᒃ|thank you|nakuʁmiːk (nakurmiik)|South Qikiqtaaluk

The app will also, simultaneously, produce markup for examples in the secondary text area (see below).

If you have more than one line of text in the text area, you must highlight the row you want to convert.

Upper-/Lower-case. Converts text to upper- or lowercase.

Transcribe to IPA. Converts Osage text to an IPA transcription. This is only a rough, phonemic transcription, and the result should be checked before use.

Vocab to Example. If you have a line of vocab data, like those shown above, this will add example markup around the term.
