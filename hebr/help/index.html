List of character apps

Updated 13-Nov-2020

This Unicode character app allows you to produce or analyse runs of Hebrew text. character apps are especially useful for people who don't know a script well, as characters are displayed in ways that aid identification.

If something is broken or missing raise an issue. For version information see the Github commit list. The pictures of the UI used in this page are taken from a variety of character apps.

Inputting characters

The app includes characters in the Unicode Hebrew block that are used for writing text in the Hebrew language.

To analyse text, copy & paste it into the large text area and select the desired control(s).

To produce Hebrew text in the text area, do one of the following:

  • Type on your keyboard, using type-assist. A quick way to input text from ASCII characters on your keyboard, based on a Latin transcription.
  • Open the shape-based input, and click on similar shapes to highlight characters you may be looking for. Good when you don't know the orthography well.
  • Click on the characters in the selection area. The blocks are arranged to assist with input, and groups of characters are arranged within a block phonetically.

The above are described in more detail elsewhere on this page.

Click on FORMAT to access and input invisible formatting characters used to control direction, joining forms, etc.

Click on EXT to also show an extended set of graphemes.

Transliterate. Converts the contents of the text area to a Latin transliteration that provides a one-to-one correspondence between Hebrew and Latin symbols.

The transliteration scheme was developed for this character app, and, where possible, uses symbols that represent sounds typically associated with their Hebrew equivalents, rather than the sometimes fairly arbitrary selection of diacritics found in other systems. Of course, the complexities of mapping graphemes to sounds means that this is far from a real phonetic transcription, and it doesn't show vowels for unvowelled text, but the process is reversible (unlike most transcriptions).

Transliterations produced this way are particularly useful for understanding the composition of text in scripts, where the individual parts are not well separated in the text.

Make vocab. This helps speed up the creation of entries for the vocabulary app. You can create a vocab item with up to 5 parts, delimited by / or |, in the following order:

  1. native text
  2. meaning
  3. transcription (IPA or other, optional)
  4. alternative transcription (if needed)
  5. notes (optional)

Hint: For RTL script character apps it often helps to set the direction of the text area to LTR. (To do this, click on the →︎ arrow below the bottom right of the text area.)

The basic transcription should be in IPA, wherever possible. You can also add an alternative transcription, such as an ISO or LOC transcription. This will be styled differently from the IPA.

If the Hebrew term has vowel diacritics, the app will automatically move the vowelled form of the term to the alternative transcription field, and will remove vowels from the initial field.

Here is an example of before and after.

אוֹפֶה/baker/oˈfe

will produce:

אופה|baker|oˈfe|אוֹפֶה

The app will also, simultaneously, produce markup for examples in the secondary text area (see below).

If you have more than one line of text in the text area, you must highlight the line you want to convert. Only convert one line at a time.

Remove vowels. Removes the vowel diacritics from the highlighted text.
